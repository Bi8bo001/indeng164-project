\author{Yu Hin Liang, Jingwen Yang \\ INDENG 164, Spring 2026}

\begin{abstract}
\small
LLM routing asks which model to call on each user prompt. We cast this problem
for an agentic AI company as a two-stage stochastic mixed-integer program. The
first stage selects a small \emph{pool} of models to maintain in production, and
the second stage routes each realised prompt to one of the selected models.
Sample-average approximation on \texttt{routerbench} (240 prompts, 32 models,
four benchmark domains) produces a tractable MILP. We add four realistic
deployment constraints (a pool-size cap, a storage budget, a per-prompt
fairness slack, and provider rules) and a distributionally robust extension
that hedges against shifts in the prompt mixture. The resulting policy traces
a smooth Pareto frontier on the cost-quality plane. At a budget of \$$0.001$
per prompt the policy reaches a weighted score of $0.967$, which already
exceeds the best single model \textsc{gpt-5} by eight percentage points at
fifty times lower cost. Saturating the budget at \$$0.005$ per prompt drives
the score to the per-prompt oracle of $0.983$, a thirteen-fold cost reduction
relative to running \textsc{gpt-5} on every prompt. A pool of $K=6$ already
captures $99.7\%$ of the achievable quality, and a $\pm 70\%$ shift in
benchmark proportions costs at most $1.2$ percentage points of worst-case
quality.
\end{abstract}
\vspace{-0.4em}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

We adopt the role of an operations-research scientist at \textit{CodePilot AI},
an agentic AI company that ships a Cursor-style coding assistant and a tutoring
sidebar for STEM students. Its workload spans code, mathematics, and knowledge
questions. For every prompt the company picks one of about thirty candidate
LLMs, ranging from \textsc{gpt-5} at \$$0.04$ per prompt down to free
open-source $9$\,B-parameter backbones. Choosing well, prompt by prompt, is
the difference between profit and loss.

Two decisions live on different time scales. \emph{Pool curation} selects
which models to maintain in production and binds for weeks because each
model carries integration, monitoring, and storage cost. \emph{Routing}
selects which model to call on a given prompt and repeats over a stream of
stochastic future requests. The best pool depends on the routing policy
that will operate on it, and the best policy in turn depends on which models
the company actually maintains. This coupling is the structure of a two-stage stochastic program
with recourse. The pool $y$ is first-stage and is committed before the
prompt distribution is realised, while the routing $x_\xi$ is second-stage
and is made after the prompt is observed. The prompt distribution
$\mathbb{P}$ is unknown and we observe only $|\pP|=240$ samples, so we use
sample-average approximation. Model correctness is itself a Bernoulli
outcome, which also makes the second-stage policy naturally stochastic.

We study two questions. \textbf{RQ1: pool curation.} How many models, and
which ones, should the company maintain under a fixed inference budget, and
are there diminishing returns? \textbf{RQ2: distributional robustness.} If
the prompt mix shifts, how much worst-case quality is lost, and is the
optimal pool stable?

\section{Data and Notation}
\label{sec:data}

The \texttt{routerbench} dataset provides binary correctness
$Q_{pm}\in\{0,1\}$ and per-prompt cost $C_{pm}\in[0,\$0.108]$ for $33$ models
on $240$ prompts. The prompts are equally split across four benchmarks
(\textsc{AIME, LCB, GPQA, MMLU-Pro}). One model
(\textsc{deepseek-v3.1-terminus}) covers only $180$ of the $240$ prompts; we
discard it and work with the $|\pM|=32$ fully-covered models to avoid ad hoc
imputation. Nine of the remaining models have $C_m=0$ because they are
open-source and can be served locally. The most expensive model is
\textsc{gpt-5}, at \$$0.0425$ per prompt and weighted score $0.888$ on the
dataset. We write $\pP_d$ for the prompt subset of domain $d\in\pD$, $w_p$ for
the SAA prompt weight (uniform $1/|\pP|$ at baseline), $S_m$ for the storage
footprint of model $m$ in GB (zero for API-only models), and use
$(B,K,S^{\max},\tau,\lambda)$ for the cost budget, pool cap, storage cap,
fairness target, and slack price.

\section{Optimization Model}
\label{sec:model}

\paragraph{Decision variables.}
The first-stage variables $y_m\in\{0,1\}$, $m\in\pM$, encode whether model $m$
is selected into the production pool. The second-stage variables
$x_{pm}\in\{0,1\}$ encode the routing decision for each prompt. A non-negative
slack $s_p\ge 0$ softens the per-prompt fairness target in cases where the
data does not allow it to be met exactly.

\paragraph{Population two-stage program.}
For a random prompt $\xi\sim\mathbb{P}$, the routing policy is a function
$x(\xi)$ from prompts to feasible assignment vectors. The population
optimisation is
\begin{align}
\max_{y,\,x(\cdot)}\
& \E_{\xi}\!\left[\textstyle\sum_{m} Q(\xi,m)\,x_m(\xi)\right]
\quad\text{s.t.}\quad
\textstyle\sum_m x_m(\xi)=1,\ \ x_m(\xi)\le y_m \ \ \text{a.s.},\nonumber\\[-1pt]
& \textstyle\sum_m y_m\le K,\ \ \sum_m S_m y_m \le S^{\max},\ \
  \E_{\xi}\!\left[\sum_m C(\xi,m)\,x_m(\xi)\right]\le B.
\end{align}
The budget is imposed in expectation rather than per-realisation to match a
monthly inference budget; $y$ is first-stage because it is committed before
any prompt is seen, and $x$ is second-stage because it depends on $\xi$.

\paragraph{Sample-average approximation (BC-2SP).}
The true distribution $\mathbb{P}$ is unknown. We replace it by the empirical
distribution on the $240$ observed prompts with weights $w_p$ and write
$x_{pm}\equiv x_m(\xi=p)$. The deterministic-equivalent MILP is
\begin{align}
\max_{x,y,s}\quad
& \sum_{p,m}\! w_p\,Q_{pm}\,x_{pm}
\,-\,\lambda\sum_p w_p\, s_p
\,-\,\varepsilon\sum_{p,m}\! w_p\,C_{pm}\,x_{pm}
\label{eq:saa}\\[-1pt]
\text{s.t.}\quad
& \textstyle\sum_m x_{pm}=1,\quad x_{pm}\le y_m, \quad \sum_m y_m\le K,
\quad \sum_m S_m y_m\le S^{\max},\nonumber\\[-1pt]
& \textstyle\sum_{p,m}\! w_p\,C_{pm}\,x_{pm}\le B,\quad
  \sum_m Q_{pm}\,x_{pm}+s_p\ge \tau,\quad
  x_{pm},y_m\in\{0,1\},\ s_p\ge 0.\nonumber
\end{align}
The first term is the SAA estimator of expected quality. The second term
softly penalises violation of the per-prompt fairness target $\tau$, with
slack price $\lambda$ chosen so that the routing prefers a correct answer
whenever one exists. The third term, with $\varepsilon=10^{-3}\!\ll\!\lambda$,
is a cost tie-breaker that selects the cheapest among quality-equivalent
solutions and removes a non-monotonicity along the upper frontier. Provider
rules are encoded by fixing the corresponding $y_m$.

\paragraph{Distributionally robust extension (DRO BC-2SP).}
The benchmark mixture $w_d:=\Pr[\xi\in\pP_d]$ is itself uncertain. We use a
relative-box ambiguity set around the empirical proportions
$w_d^{0}=|\pP_d|/|\pP|$, namely
$\pU(\delta)=\{w\in\Delta_{|\pD|}:\ |w_d-w_d^{0}|\le \delta\,w_d^{0}\ \forall d\}$.
Let $f_d(x):=\tfrac{1}{|\pP_d|}\sum_{p\in\pP_d,m} Q_{pm}\,x_{pm}$ be the
conditional expected score on domain $d$. The DRO objective is
$\max_{y,x}\min_{w\in\pU(\delta)}\sum_d w_d f_d(x)$. Reparametrising
$w_d=w_d^{0}(1+e_d)$ with $|e_d|\le\delta$ and $\sum_d w_d^{0} e_d=0$, the
inner LP admits the closed-form dual
$\min_{w}\sum_d w_d f_d(x)=\sum_d w_d^{0} f_d(x)
   -\delta\cdot\min_\lambda\sum_d w_d^{0}|f_d(x)-\lambda|$,
where the inner minimiser $\lambda^\star$ is the $w^{0}$-weighted median of
$\{f_d(x)\}$. Linearising the absolute values with $z_d\ge 0$ and a free
$\lambda$ gives the equivalent MILP
\begin{equation}
\max_{x,y,\lambda,z}\
\sum_d w_d^{0} f_d(x)\,-\,\delta\sum_d w_d^{0} z_d
\quad\text{s.t.}\quad
z_d\ge f_d(x)-\lambda,\quad z_d\ge \lambda-f_d(x), \ \ \forall d\in\pD,
\label{eq:dro_milp}
\end{equation}
together with the BC-2SP constraints in~\eqref{eq:saa} and the budget under
nominal $w$. The reformulation adds only $|\pD|+1$ continuous variables and
$2|\pD|$ inequality constraints, so it is essentially the same size as BC-2SP.

\paragraph{Companion formulations.}
Three variants share the same constraint set: \emph{QC-2SP} minimises
expected cost subject to a target weighted score $Q^{\min}$ (the dual side
of the same frontier); \emph{Randomised BC-2SP} relaxes $x_{pm}\in[0,1]$
into a stochastic policy with $\Pr[\xi=p\mapsto m]=x_{pm}$; \emph{Cascade
BC-2SP} pairs $(m_1,m_2)$ per prompt with a zero-cost verifier and escalates
only on failure, giving a MILP of size $|\pP||\pM|^2$.

\paragraph{Solving the MILP.}
The BC-2SP MILP has $8{,}224$ binary variables and $8{,}163$ linear
constraints. We implement BC-2SP, QC-2SP, DRO BC-2SP and the cascade variant
in Pyomo with the HiGHS solver; a single instance solves in two to four
seconds, and a full eleven-point frontier sweep completes in under one minute.
Storage parameters $S_m$ are heuristics from the model name: roughly $14$ to
$18$\,GB for $7$ to $9$\,B open-source backbones, $0$\,GB for provider-hosted
APIs, and about $470$\,GB for the $235$\,B Qwen mixture-of-experts. The
notebook \texttt{solution.ipynb} accompanies this report.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}

All experiments use uniform empirical weights $w_p=1/240$,
$\varepsilon=10^{-3}$, and $\lambda=1$ unless otherwise stated.

\paragraph{Cost-quality Pareto frontier.}
We initially adopted the weighted-sum scalarisation
$\min\,\mathrm{cost}-\alpha\cdot\mathrm{score}$ suggested by the starter
code, but found that $\alpha$ has no operational meaning and that combining
it with a fairness penalty collapses the pool to two or three models at
small $\alpha$. Switching to an explicit budget $B$ removes both issues:
the budget has clear monetary meaning, and sweeping it from $\$10^{-5}$ to
$\$5\!\times\!10^{-3}$ at $K=8$ traces the BC-2SP frontier of
Figure~\ref{fig:frontier}, which strictly dominates every Single-Best
baseline. At $B=\$10^{-5}$ the
policy already scores $0.892$, marginally above \textsc{gpt-5} but at four
orders of magnitude lower cost, because the optimal pool concentrates on free
open-source backbones. At $B=\$10^{-3}$ the score reaches $0.967$, which is
eight percentage points above \textsc{gpt-5} at fifty times lower cost. At
$B=\$5\!\times\!10^{-3}$ the policy attains the per-prompt oracle of $0.983$
at actual cost \$$0.0032$ per prompt, a $13.1\!\times\!$ cost reduction
relative to \textsc{gpt-5}. Beyond this budget the score saturates because no
further quality is available in the data.

\paragraph{RQ1: diminishing returns of pool size.}
Fixing $B=\$5\!\times\!10^{-3}$ and sweeping $K$ from $1$ to $10$
(Figure~\ref{fig:K}) shows a fast-then-saturating curve. A single model
achieves only $0.742$ because no model alone covers all four benchmarks
adequately. Adding a second model already lifts the score to $0.892$
(a fifteen percentage-point gain). The curve continues to rise but flattens
quickly: $K=4$ reaches $0.946$, $K=6$ reaches $0.979$ ($99.6\%$ of the
$K=10$ value), and from $K=7$ on the score is pinned at the oracle
$0.983$. The operationally relevant message is that six to seven well-chosen
models are sufficient regardless of how many candidates the company nominally
has access to; further models are pure overhead.

\paragraph{Pool composition and traffic concentration.}
Figure~\ref{fig:pool} shows the growth path of the optimal pool. The
optimiser first picks complementary free open-source backbones, then admits a
flagship model only at $K=4$, and uses mid-tier APIs to fill in between. In
the converged $K=8$ pool, \textsc{gpt-5} routes only $1.7\%$ of traffic and
serves as a backstop for the four hardest prompts that no smaller model can
solve. The free \textsc{GLM-Z1-9B-0414} alone handles $57.5\%$ of prompts.
The remaining traffic is distributed across \textsc{kimi-k2-0905} ($10.0\%$),
\textsc{Intern-S1-mini} ($8.3\%$), \textsc{Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct} ($7.1\%$),
\textsc{glm-4.6} ($5.8\%$), \textsc{gemini-2.5-flash} ($5.4\%$), and
\textsc{qwen3-235b-thinking-2507} ($4.2\%$). The traffic is therefore not
concentrated on the strongest single model; the optimal policy is mostly
flat with a flagship as backstop.

\paragraph{Per-benchmark breakdown.}
At $B=\$0.005$, $K=8$ the policy solves \textsc{LCB} and \textsc{GPQA}
perfectly and reaches $0.967$ on both \textsc{AIME} and \textsc{MMLU-Pro}.
The four remaining prompts are exactly those for which no model in the data
produces a correct answer, so the routing matches the per-prompt oracle on
every solvable prompt.

\paragraph{RQ2: distributional robustness.}
Sweeping $\delta$ from $0$ to $0.7$ at $B=\$0.005$, $K=8$
(Figure~\ref{fig:dro}), the nominal score remains pinned at $0.983$.
The worst-case score from~\eqref{eq:dro_milp} drops by $0.50$, $0.83$, and
$1.17$ percentage points at $\delta=0.3$, $0.5$, $0.7$. The DRO pool
overlaps with the nominal in seven of eight slots for $\delta\le 0.5$, so
the pool itself is highly stable. The price of distributional robustness on
this dataset is therefore small.

\paragraph{Constraint ablations and formulation comparison.}
Storage caps down to $50$\,GB are free; tightening to $20$\,GB costs
$1.2$\,pp because the optimiser falls back from $\sim\!8$\,B backbones onto
smaller alternatives. Excluding $\{\textsc{gpt-5},\textsc{gpt-5-chat}\}$
costs only $0.4$\,pp ($0.983\!\to\!0.979$ at \$$0.0024$) since
\textsc{qwen3-235b-thinking-2507} and \textsc{kimi-k2-0905} absorb the slack;
forcing \textsc{claude-sonnet-4} preserves the score at $38\%$ more cost.
Across formulations, BC-2SP and QC-2SP coincide at the corresponding
$(B, Q^{\min})$ pair as a primal-dual pair on the same frontier, the DRO
variant trades a small cost increase for $\delta$-protection, and the
randomised and cascade variants give only marginal lift on this binary-score
dataset (randomised: $+0.35$\,pp at $B\!=\!\$0.001$; cascade: matches BC-2SP
at $B\!=\!\$0.0005$).

\section{Discussion and Recommendations}
\label{sec:discuss}

\paragraph{Answer to RQ1.}
A pool of $K^\star=6$ is sufficient and $K=7$ already saturates at the
oracle. The pool the optimiser produces is heterogeneous and not concentrated:
\textsc{gpt-5} routes only $1.7\%$ of traffic, while free open-source
backbones together handle about $60\%$. The dominant strategy on this
benchmark is therefore to route most prompts through free models and reserve
a single flagship as a backstop for the small set of hard prompts.

\paragraph{Answer to RQ2.}
The price of distributional robustness is small. The worst-case score drops
by at most $1.2$\,pp when each benchmark proportion swings by $\pm 70\%$,
and the optimal pool overlaps in seven of eight slots with the nominal
solution for $\delta\le 0.5$. Since the DRO MILP is the same size as BC-2SP,
deploying it by default is essentially free insurance against mix shifts.

\paragraph{Recommendations.}
Maintain a six-model pool combining a flagship backstop, a mid-tier API, a
reasoning-focused model, and two or three free $\sim\!8$\,B open-source
backbones (concretely: \textsc{gpt-5}, \textsc{gemini-2.5-flash},
\textsc{kimi-k2-0905}, \textsc{GLM-Z1-9B-0414}, \textsc{Intern-S1-mini},
\textsc{Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct}). Re-solve the SAA MILP whenever cost or
quality targets change, treat fairness as soft via $s_p$, and budget
$50$\,GB of local storage.

\paragraph{Limitations and future work.}
Binary correctness underuses information; graded scores would let the
randomised and cascade variants dominate by a larger margin. Latency and
log-loss objectives drop into the SAA MILP without difficulty, and an
online-learning loop that updates $\hat{\mathbb{P}}$ from production traffic
would close the deployment loop.

\paragraph{Personal takeaway.}
The striking moment was watching a routing policy thirteen times cheaper
than \textsc{gpt-5} attain its oracle of $0.983$. Treating the data as a
$240\!\times\!32$ matrix of $(Q,C)$ pairs immediately suggested the two-stage
form, and HiGHS solved every instance in seconds, keeping modelling and
analysis fast enough to be genuinely fun.
