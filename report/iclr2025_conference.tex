\title{Curating a Robust LLM Pool: A Two-Stage Stochastic Optimization Approach for Cost-Constrained LLM Routing}
\author{Jingwen Yang, Yu Hin Liang \\ INDENG 164, Spring 2026}
\date{}

\begin{document}\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
\small
LLM routing asks which model to call on each user prompt. We cast this problem
for an agentic AI company as a two-stage stochastic mixed-integer program. The
first stage selects a small \emph{pool} of models to maintain in production, and
the second stage routes each realised prompt to one of the selected models.
Sample-average approximation on \texttt{routerbench} (240 prompts, 32 models,
four benchmark domains) produces a tractable MILP. We add four realistic
deployment constraints (a pool-size cap, a storage budget, a per-prompt
fairness slack, and provider rules) and a distributionally robust extension
that hedges against shifts in the prompt mixture. The resulting policy traces
a smooth Pareto frontier on the cost-quality plane. At a budget of \$$0.001$
per prompt the policy reaches a weighted score of $0.967$, which already
exceeds the best single model \textsc{gpt-5} by eight percentage points at
fifty times lower cost. Saturating the budget at \$$0.005$ per prompt drives
the score to the per-prompt oracle of $0.983$, a thirteen-fold cost reduction
relative to running \textsc{gpt-5} on every prompt. A pool of $K=6$ already
captures $99.7\%$ of the achievable quality, and a $\pm 70\%$ shift in
benchmark proportions costs at most $1.2$ percentage points of worst-case
quality. Code is available at
\url{https://github.com/Bi8bo001/indeng164-project}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

We adopt the role of an operations-research scientist at \textit{CodePilot AI},
an agentic AI company that ships a Cursor-style coding assistant and a tutoring
sidebar for STEM students. Its workload spans code, mathematics, and knowledge
questions. For every prompt the company picks one of about thirty candidate
LLMs, ranging from \textsc{gpt-5} at \$$0.04$ per prompt down to free
open-source $9$\,B-parameter backbones. Choosing well, prompt by prompt, is
the difference between profit and loss.

Two decisions live on different time scales. \emph{Pool curation} selects
which models to maintain in production and binds for weeks because each
model carries integration, monitoring, and storage cost. \emph{Routing}
selects which model to call on a given prompt and repeats over a stream of
stochastic future requests. The best pool depends on the routing policy
that will operate on it, and the best policy in turn depends on which
models the company actually maintains. This coupling is the structure of a
two-stage stochastic program with recourse~\citep{birge1997introduction}:
the pool $y$ is first-stage and is committed before the prompt distribution
is realised, while the routing $x_\xi$ is second-stage and is made after
each prompt is observed. The prompt distribution $\mathbb{P}$ itself is
unknown, and we observe only $|\pP|=240$ samples from it, so we approximate
expectations through sample-average approximation.

Two questions then drive the report. They are precisely the questions a
deployment manager would ask before signing off on a routing system:
\textbf{RQ1: pool curation.} How many models, and which ones, should the
company maintain under a fixed inference budget, and are there diminishing
returns as the pool grows? \textbf{RQ2: distributional robustness.} If the
prompt mix shifts in production, how much worst-case quality is lost, and
is the optimal pool stable across such shifts?

\section{Data and Notation}
\label{sec:data}

The \texttt{routerbench} dataset~\citep{li2026routerbench} provides binary
correctness $Q_{pm}\in\{0,1\}$ and per-prompt cost $C_{pm}\in[0,\$0.108]$
for $33$ models on $240$ prompts. The prompts are equally split across four benchmarks
(\textsc{AIME, LCB, GPQA, MMLU-Pro}). One model
(\textsc{deepseek-v3.1-terminus}~\citep{liu2024deepseek}) covers only $180$
of the $240$ prompts; we discard it and work with the $|\pM|=32$
fully-covered models to avoid ad hoc imputation. Nine of the remaining models have $C_m=0$ because they are
open-source and can be served locally. The most expensive model is
\textsc{gpt-5}, at \$$0.0425$ per prompt and weighted score $0.888$ on the
dataset. We write $\pP_d$ for the prompt subset of domain $d\in\pD$, $w_p$ for
the SAA prompt weight (uniform $1/|\pP|$ at baseline), $S_m$ for the storage
footprint of model $m$ in GB (zero for API-only models), and use
$(B,K,S^{\max},\tau,\lambda)$ for the cost budget, pool cap, storage cap,
fairness target, and slack price.

\section{Optimization Model}
\label{sec:model}

\paragraph{Decision variables.}
The first-stage variables $y_m\in\{0,1\}$, $m\in\pM$, encode whether model $m$
is selected into the production pool. The second-stage variables
$x_{pm}\in\{0,1\}$ encode the routing decision for each prompt. A non-negative
slack $s_p\ge 0$ softens the per-prompt fairness target in cases where the
data does not allow it to be met exactly.

\paragraph{Population two-stage program.}
For a random prompt $\xi\sim\mathbb{P}$, the routing policy is a function
$x(\xi)$ from prompts to feasible assignment vectors. The population
optimisation is
\begin{align}
\max_{y,\,x(\cdot)}\
& \E_{\xi}\!\left[\textstyle\sum_{m} Q(\xi,m)\,x_m(\xi)\right]
\quad\text{s.t.}\quad
\textstyle\sum_m x_m(\xi)=1,\ \ x_m(\xi)\le y_m \ \ \text{a.s.},\nonumber\\[-1pt]
& \textstyle\sum_m y_m\le K,\ \ \sum_m S_m y_m \le S^{\max},\ \
  \E_{\xi}\!\left[\sum_m C(\xi,m)\,x_m(\xi)\right]\le B.
\end{align}
The budget is imposed in expectation rather than per-realisation to match a
monthly inference budget; $y$ is first-stage because it is committed before
any prompt is seen, and $x$ is second-stage because it depends on $\xi$.

\paragraph{Sample-average approximation (BC-2SP).}
The true distribution $\mathbb{P}$ is unknown, so we apply sample-average
approximation~\citep{shapiro2021lectures}. We replace $\mathbb{P}$ by the
empirical distribution on the $240$ observed prompts with weights $w_p$ and
write $x_{pm}\equiv x_m(\xi=p)$. The deterministic-equivalent MILP is
\begin{align}
\max_{x,y,s}\quad
& \sum_{p,m}\! w_p\,Q_{pm}\,x_{pm}
\,-\,\lambda\sum_p w_p\, s_p
\,-\,\varepsilon\sum_{p,m}\! w_p\,C_{pm}\,x_{pm}
\label{eq:saa}\\[-1pt]
\text{s.t.}\quad
& \textstyle\sum_m x_{pm}=1,\quad x_{pm}\le y_m, \quad \sum_m y_m\le K,
\quad \sum_m S_m y_m\le S^{\max},\nonumber\\[-1pt]
& \textstyle\sum_{p,m}\! w_p\,C_{pm}\,x_{pm}\le B,\quad
  \sum_m Q_{pm}\,x_{pm}+s_p\ge \tau,\quad
  x_{pm},y_m\in\{0,1\},\ s_p\ge 0.\nonumber
\end{align}
The first term is the SAA estimator of expected quality. The second term
softly penalises violation of the per-prompt fairness target $\tau$, with
slack price $\lambda$ chosen so that the routing prefers a correct answer
whenever one exists. The third term, with $\varepsilon=10^{-3}\!\ll\!\lambda$,
is a cost tie-breaker that selects the cheapest among quality-equivalent
solutions and removes a non-monotonicity along the upper frontier. Provider
rules are encoded by fixing the corresponding $y_m$.

\paragraph{Distributionally robust extension (DRO BC-2SP).}
The empirical proportions $w_d^{0}=|\pP_d|/|\pP|$ are themselves a noisy
estimate of the production benchmark mixture
$w_d:=\Pr[\xi\in\pP_d]$, and a router that is tuned to $w^{0}$ may degrade
under a future shift. Motivated by distributionally robust
optimisation more generally~\citep{esfahani2018data}, we hedge against
this by modelling the true mixture as lying in a simple
\emph{relative-box} ambiguity set (rather than a Wasserstein ball)
$\pU(\delta)=\{w\in\Delta_{|\pD|}:\ |w_d-w_d^{0}|\le \delta\,w_d^{0}\ \forall d\in\pD\}$
around $w^{0}$, while keeping prompts within each domain uniformly
weighted. Writing $f_d(x):=\tfrac{1}{|\pP_d|}\sum_{p\in\pP_d,m} Q_{pm}\,x_{pm}$
for the conditional expected score on domain $d$, the DRO objective becomes
$\max_{y,x}\min_{w\in\pU(\delta)}\sum_d w_d f_d(x)$.

The inner minimisation over $w$ is a small LP that admits a closed form.
Reparametrising $w_d=w_d^{0}(1+e_d)$ with $|e_d|\le\delta$ and
$\sum_d w_d^{0} e_d=0$ and applying LP duality gives
\begin{equation}
\min_{w\in\pU(\delta)}\sum_d w_d f_d(x)
\,=\,\sum_d w_d^{0} f_d(x)\,-\,
   \delta\cdot\min_{\lambda\in\R}\sum_d w_d^{0}\,|f_d(x)-\lambda|,
\end{equation}
where the inner minimiser $\lambda^{\star}$ is the $w^{0}$-weighted median
of $\{f_d(x)\}$. Linearising the absolute value with non-negative
auxiliaries $z_d$ and a free $\lambda$ turns the outer max into the MILP
\begin{equation}
\max_{x,y,\lambda,z}\
\sum_d w_d^{0} f_d(x)\,-\,\delta\sum_d w_d^{0} z_d
\quad\text{s.t.}\quad
z_d\ge f_d(x)-\lambda,\ \ z_d\ge \lambda-f_d(x), \ \ \forall d\in\pD,
\label{eq:dro_milp}
\end{equation}
together with the BC-2SP constraints in~\eqref{eq:saa} and the budget
imposed under the nominal $w$. The reformulation adds only $|\pD|+1$
continuous variables and $2|\pD|$ inequality constraints, so DRO BC-2SP is
essentially the same size as BC-2SP.

\paragraph{Companion formulations.}
We also consider three variants that share the constraint set of
BC-2SP and stress different aspects of the trade-off. \emph{QC-2SP}
minimises expected cost subject to a target weighted score $Q^{\min}$, and
traces the same Pareto frontier from the dual side. \emph{Randomised
BC-2SP} relaxes $x_{pm}\in[0,1]$, so that the recourse becomes an explicit
stochastic policy that selects model $m$ on prompt $p$ with probability
$x_{pm}$. \emph{Cascade BC-2SP} pairs each prompt with a tuple
$(m_1,m_2)$ and a zero-cost verifier: model $m_1$ is invoked first, and the
system escalates to $m_2$ only on failure, which gives a MILP with
$|\pP||\pM|^2$ binary variables.

\paragraph{Solving the MILP.}
The BC-2SP MILP has $8{,}224$ binary variables and $8{,}163$ linear
constraints. We implement BC-2SP, QC-2SP, DRO BC-2SP and the cascade variant
in Pyomo~\citep{bynum2021pyomo} with the HiGHS solver~\citep{huangfu2018parallelizing};
a single instance solves in two to four seconds, and a full eleven-point
frontier sweep completes in under one minute.
Storage parameters $S_m$ are heuristics from the model name: roughly $14$ to
$18$\,GB for $7$ to $9$\,B open-source backbones, $0$\,GB for provider-hosted
APIs, and about $470$\,GB for the $235$\,B Qwen mixture-of-experts. The
notebook \texttt{solution.ipynb} accompanies this report.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}



All experiments use uniform empirical weights $w_p=1/240$,
$\varepsilon=10^{-3}$, $\lambda=1$, and $\tau=1$ unless otherwise stated;
under $\tau=1$ the slack $s_p$ records whether prompt $p$ is left unsolved
by its assigned model.

\paragraph{Cost-quality Pareto frontier.}
We initially adopted the weighted-sum scalarisation
$\min\,\mathrm{cost}-\alpha\cdot\mathrm{score}$ suggested by the starter
code, but found that $\alpha$ has no operational meaning and that combining
it with a fairness penalty collapses the pool to two or three models at
small $\alpha$. Switching to an explicit budget $B$ removes both issues:
the budget has clear monetary meaning, and sweeping it from $\$10^{-5}$ to
$\$5\!\times\!10^{-3}$ at $K=8$ traces the BC-2SP frontier of
Figure~\ref{fig:frontier}, which strictly dominates every Single-Best
baseline. At $B=\$10^{-5}$ the
policy already scores $0.892$, marginally above \textsc{gpt-5} but at four
orders of magnitude lower cost, because the optimal pool concentrates on free
open-source backbones. At $B=\$10^{-3}$ the score reaches $0.967$, which is
eight percentage points above \textsc{gpt-5} at fifty times lower cost. At
$B=\$5\!\times\!10^{-3}$ the policy attains the per-prompt oracle quality
of $0.983$ at actual cost \$$0.0032$ per prompt, a $13.1\!\times\!$ cost
reduction
relative to \textsc{gpt-5}. Beyond this budget the score saturates because no
further quality is available in the data. Table~\ref{tab:headline}
summarises the headline operating points.

\paragraph{RQ1: diminishing returns of pool size.}
Fixing $B=\$5\!\times\!10^{-3}$ and sweeping $K$ from $1$ to $10$
(Figure~\ref{fig:K}) shows a fast-then-saturating curve. Under this
budget a single-model pool achieves only $0.742$, because the strongest
single model \textsc{gpt-5} is not budget-feasible as an always-on choice
(it costs $\$0.0425$ per prompt) and no budget-feasible single model
covers all four benchmarks adequately. Adding a second model already lifts
the score to $0.892$ (a fifteen percentage-point gain). The curve continues to rise but flattens
quickly: $K=4$ reaches $0.946$, $K=6$ reaches $0.979$ ($99.6\%$ of the
$K=10$ value), and from $K=7$ on the score is pinned at the oracle
$0.983$. The operationally relevant message is that six to seven well-chosen
models are sufficient regardless of how many candidates the company nominally
has access to; further models are pure overhead.

\paragraph{Pool composition and traffic concentration.}
The growth path of the optimal pool, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pool}, is
itself informative. The optimiser first selects complementary free
open-source backbones; only at $K=4$ does it admit a flagship; and at
larger $K$ it fills in with mid-tier APIs. The composition is therefore not
``small models on top of one big model'' but the reverse: a mostly free
base, with a flagship pulled in only when the data demands it.

The traffic distribution at $K=8$ tells the same story from a different
angle. The single most-used model is the free
\textsc{GLM-Z1-9B-0414}~\citep{glm2024chatglm}, which alone handles
$57.5\%$ of prompts. The remaining traffic spreads across six models, with
no single model exceeding $11\%$: \textsc{kimi-k2-0905} ($10.0\%$),
\textsc{Intern-S1-mini} ($8.3\%$),
\textsc{Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct}~\citep{grattafiori2024llama} ($7.1\%$),
\textsc{glm-4.6} ($5.8\%$), \textsc{gemini-2.5-flash} ($5.4\%$), and
\textsc{qwen3-235b-thinking-2507}~\citep{yang2025qwen3} ($4.2\%$).
\textsc{gpt-5} sits at the bottom of this list at $1.7\%$, used only as a
backstop on the four hardest prompts that no smaller model can solve. The
optimal routing is therefore flat-with-a-backstop rather than concentrated
on the strongest single model.

\paragraph{RQ2: distributional robustness.}
Sweeping $\delta$ from $0$ to $0.7$ at $B=\$0.005$, $K=8$
(Figure~\ref{fig:dro}), the nominal score remains pinned at $0.983$.
The worst-case score from~\eqref{eq:dro_milp} drops by $0.50$, $0.83$, and
$1.17$ percentage points at $\delta=0.3$, $0.5$, $0.7$. The DRO pool
overlaps with the nominal in seven of eight slots for $\delta\le 0.5$, so
the pool itself is highly stable. The price of distributional robustness on
this dataset is therefore small.

\paragraph{Constraint ablations and formulation comparison.}
The realistic constraints behave as one would hope. Storage caps down to
$50$\,GB are free, while tightening to $20$\,GB costs $1.2$\,pp because
the optimiser falls back from $\sim\!8$\,B-parameter open-source backbones
onto smaller alternatives. Excluding the OpenAI flagship pair costs only
$0.4$\,pp and actually \emph{lowers} cost to \$$0.0024$ per prompt, because
\textsc{qwen3-235b-thinking-2507} and \textsc{kimi-k2-0905} absorb most of
the lost quality; mandating \textsc{claude-sonnet-4} preserves the score
but raises cost by $38\%$. Across formulations, BC-2SP and QC-2SP coincide
at the corresponding $(B, Q^{\min})$ pair as expected for a primal-dual
pair on the same Pareto frontier, the DRO variant pays a small cost
premium for $\delta$-protection, and the randomised and cascade variants
give only marginal lift on this binary-score dataset (randomised
$+0.35$\,pp at $B\!=\!\$0.001$; cascade matches BC-2SP at $B\!=\!\$0.0005$).
Finer-grained graded scores would amplify the latter two in production.

\section{Discussion and Recommendations}
\label{sec:discuss}

\paragraph{Answer to RQ1.}
A pool of $K^{\star}=6$ models is sufficient: it captures $99.7\%$ of the
quality of any larger pool we tested, and adding a seventh model already
pushes the score onto the per-prompt oracle quality. Crucially, the pool that the
optimiser produces is heterogeneous and not concentrated. \textsc{gpt-5}
routes only $1.7\%$ of traffic, while free open-source backbones together
handle about $60\%$. The cost-quality optimum on this benchmark is
therefore obtained by routing most prompts through free models and
reserving a single flagship as a backstop for the small set of hard
prompts that no smaller model can solve.

\paragraph{Answer to RQ2.}
The cost of robustifying against benchmark-mix shifts is also small. Even
when each benchmark proportion is allowed to swing by $\pm 70\%$, the
worst-case score of the DRO solution drops by at most $1.2$\,pp; and for
$\delta\le 0.5$ the DRO pool overlaps with the nominal solution in seven
of eight slots. Because the DRO MILP is essentially the same size as
BC-2SP, deploying the DRO solution by default is essentially free
insurance against mix shifts.

\paragraph{Recommendations.}
We recommend $K=6$ as the cost-conscious production default since it
already captures $99.7\%$ of the achievable quality and saves the overhead
of an extra slot; the figures and tables use $K=8$ only as a diagnostic
upper-frontier setting that matches the oracle quality exactly. A concrete
instance of the six-model pool combines a flagship backstop, a mid-tier
API, a reasoning model, and two or three free $\sim\!8$\,B open-source
backbones, e.g.\ $\{$\textsc{gpt-5}, \textsc{gemini-2.5-flash},
\textsc{kimi-k2-0905}, \textsc{GLM-Z1-9B-0414}, \textsc{Intern-S1-mini},
\textsc{Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct}$\}$. The SAA MILP solves in seconds, so the
team should re-solve it whenever the cost or quality target changes rather
than freezing the policy; fairness should be enforced softly through
$s_p$ so that unsolvable prompts are absorbed by the slack; and a storage
budget of $50$\,GB is sufficient (tighter caps cost about $1.2$\,pp).

\paragraph{Limitations and future work.}
We follow the dataset convention and treat the nine open-source models as
having zero per-prompt API cost; in practice, GPU serving carries
amortised hardware, latency, and maintenance cost that could be added as a
separate cost term, on top of the storage cap we already model. Beyond
that, binary correctness underuses information that graded scores would
expose, which is precisely the regime where the randomised and cascade
variants should outperform deterministic routing. Latency and log-loss
objectives drop into the SAA MILP without difficulty, and an
online-learning loop that updates $\hat{\mathbb{P}}$ from production
traffic would let the router drift with the user base rather than freezing
on historical samples.

\paragraph{Personal takeaway.}
Before this project we only had the rough intuition that small models
should handle easy prompts and stronger ones be reserved for hard
prompts. Writing out the two-stage formulation made this concrete:
pool selection and per-prompt routing are two distinct decisions, and
separating them showed us where the cost savings actually come from.
The most surprising result was that a six-model pool nearly matches the
per-prompt oracle quality at a small fraction of \textsc{gpt-5}'s cost,
and HiGHS solving each instance in seconds made the work genuinely fun.
